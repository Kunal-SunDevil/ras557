Bipedal Robot Using 5-Bar Mechanism

Project Overview

This project focuses on the development of a bipedal robot using a 5-bar linkage mechanism. Inspired by the biomechanics of bird locomotion, the robot mimics natural gait patterns such as walking and hopping. Using a five-bar linkage system leveraging the foldable mechanism, the biomimicry of an ostrich's legs mimics the intricate, multi-joint motions of real limbs, offering important insights into bipedal biomechanics. This design makes smoother and more flexible motion possible in robotic applications, which captures the kinematics and dynamics of different gaits. Biped biomechanism has been a hot research topic for decades thanks to its adaptability and low energy required to walk and get higher mobility. This project aims to study, develop, and mimic bipedal gait using foldable robotic techniques to further the utility and understanding of the nuances of foldable mechanismsâ€™ structural rigidity and the flexibility of joints and links.

Objectives

  • Analyze and replicate bird-like gaits using foldable mechanisms.
  • Simulate kinematics and dynamics using MuJoCo.
  • Optimize end-effector link length for efficient locomotion.
  • Build a bipedal robot.
  • Compare and validate the simulation results with prototype performance.

Methodology

  1. Design a foldable 5-bar mechanism for the bipedal robot.
  2. Conduct simulations in MuJoCo to generate and analyze gait patterns.
  3. Fabricate a robot body using lightweight materials like fiberglass.
  4. Control the robot using servo motors and a microcontroller.

Manufacturing Planning

Design Process

The bipedal robot features a modular design architecture that can be broken down into three primary components: the main body structure, the five-bar leg mechanism, and the end-effector knee, each fabricated independently to ensure precision and ease of modification. This modular approach was specifically engineered to facilitate comprehensive research on end-effector length effects. The legs were designed with quick-release mechanisms that seamlessly interface with the primary five-bar mechanism, while the motors remain securely mounted to the robot's structural plane.

This thoughtful design architecture offers several significant advantages:

  1. Experimental flexibility: The modular components allow researchers to swap different leg configurations while maintaining consistent motor positioning and control parameters, enabling precise comparative analysis of end-effector performance.
  2. Mechanical stability: The strategic mounting of motors directly to the structural plane minimizes unwanted vibrations and ensures precise control over leg movements, regardless of end-effector length variations.
  3. Systematic data collection: The ability to rapidly interchange leg lengths while keeping other variables constant facilitates methodical investigation of how end-effector length influences critical performance metrics, including energy efficiency, gait stability, and dynamic response.
  4. Design optimization: Independent fabrication of each component not only simplifies maintenance and replacement but also enables iterative testing of various configurations to determine optimal setups for specific locomotion tasks or environmental conditions.
  5. Assembly efficiency: The separation of components into distinct modules streamlines both the initial assembly process and subsequent modifications, reducing downtime between experimental trials and facilitating rapid prototyping of new design iterations.

Manufacturing Process

Bipedal Robot rev 1.0

For the initial prototype fabrication, a precise five-layer laminate structure was designed to achieve optimal mechanical properties. The structure begins with a rigid cardstock base layer that provides fundamental structural support, followed by an adhesive layer ensuring secure bonding. The critical middle layer consists of a thin plastic film, specifically engineered for creating flexible joints within the mechanism. This flexible layer is then secured by a second adhesive layer, and the structure is completed with another cardstock layer, forming a symmetrical and robust sandwich configuration.

This carefully engineered layering system creates a balance between structural integrity and controlled flexibility, where the outer cardstock layers provide rigidity while the central plastic film enables precise joint movement at designated locations.

Following the established manufacturing workflow, each layer is modified with simple plain hinges at joint locations to facilitate interconnection between different body segments. The individual layer designs are illustrated below:

The assembly process of the robot begins with integrating the fabricated laminate structures into a functional mechanism. The body laminate serves as the central mounting platform, where servo adapters are securely fastened using nuts and bolts at precisely designated locations shown in the previous illustration.

Snap connectors are strategically positioned and attached at specific points on the body laminate, creating secure attachment points that facilitate the integration of the five-bar mechanism and leg components with the main body. These snap connectors are essential as they allow for both robust mechanical connection and easy disassembly when needed, enhancing the modularity and maintainability of the robot design.

Bipedal Robot rev 2.0

Initial testing of the cardstock-based robot prototype revealed significant structural limitations that impacted its performance. The joints and links exhibited vulnerability to failure during operation, and the structure lacked sufficient rigidity to maintain stable positioning. Most notably, the robot body demonstrated undesirable flexing during servo motor actuation, compromising precise control and movement accuracy. To address these structural deficiencies, we implemented a comprehensive material upgrade by transitioning to a more robust five-layer fiberglass laminate design.

This enhanced material composition delivered substantial improvements in mechanical performance, providing superior rigidity and enabling the robot to maintain stable positioning with improved self-supporting capabilities. However, this material transition presented both advantages and challenges. While the fiberglass structure significantly enhanced the robot's rigidity and structural integrity, it also introduced several trade-offs. The overall weight of the robot increased notably due to the denser material composition. The original snap connector system had to be replaced with a more robust nut-and-bolt assembly method to accommodate the increased structural demands while preserving the design's modularity. Additionally, the cost of fabrication increased substantially due to the more expensive materials and complex manufacturing process required for fiberglass laminates. This evolution in design represents a careful balance between structural performance and practical considerations, where enhanced mechanical properties were achieved at the expense of increased weight, assembly complexity, and manufacturing cost.

While maintaining the same design files and fabrication process from the previous version, we optimized the manufacturing approach for efficiency. The five-bar leg mechanism was constructed using the complete five-layer laminate structure, whereas the body was simplified to a two-layer design. The top and bottom layers of the body were individually laser cut and then connected using standoffs, maintaining structural integrity while reducing production time. This hybrid approach preserved the design's core functionality while streamlining the manufacturing process, effectively balancing structural requirements with fabrication efficiency.
